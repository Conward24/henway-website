# Henway Marketing QA Rubric

> A reusable checklist for writing and reviewing any Henway page, section, or post.
> Distilled from a marketing/UX working session (originally on MyLÚA) — the same rules
> apply to every Henway property. Run new copy against this before it ships.
>
> **North Star:** make it instantly digestible for a layperson. If a normal person has to
> stop and figure out "wait, what is this / what does that word mean," you've already lost them.

---

## The 8 rules

### 1. Land the human moment before the mechanism
Open with the reader's lived moment or desire, not with what the product *is*. Emotion first,
tech later. Once they feel "that's me," they'll follow you into the details.
- **Test:** Does sentence one make the reader feel seen, or does it describe the company?
- **Good (ours):** "Which AI tool should you build with?" (their exact question)
- **Weak:** "Product Creation Platform" / "Turn ideas into real AI products" (category + capability)

### 2. Name the enemy, then flip it
Say what everything else does wrong, *then* show how Henway is different. The contrast is what
makes the value land. Don't just claim you're good — show the broken status quo first.
- **Test:** Is there a villain (the blank page, the tool maze, the research week) before the promise?
- **Good (ours):** "Too many tools. A blank screen. Lost weeks." → then the answer.

### 3. Kill jargon and vague words
If a layperson doesn't instantly get a word, cut it or translate it. Use concrete,
positive-forward language.
- **Test:** Would a smart non-technical friend need to ask "what does that mean?"
- **Cut / translate:** "generally available" → "live / free today"; "platform" (alone),
  "infrastructure," "smart systems," "agentic" → say what it *does* in plain words.

### 4. One sentence, then 3–5 *tangible* things
After the hook, prove it with concrete, pictureable features — not categories.
- **Test:** Can the reader picture each item? "A calendar to manage my schedule" beats "workflow tools."
- **Good (ours):** "Four things you can use today" with the actual deliverables named.

### 5. Don't make the reader carry load
Once you've landed a point, stop. Cut the paragraph that re-explains it. Get technical only
*after* you've earned the reader's attention.
- **Test:** Can you delete this sentence and lose nothing? Then delete it.

### 6. Resolve the category fast — "what even IS this?"
Within the first beat, make the basic shape unmistakable (a free app? a studio? a tool?).
Ambiguity is a tax the reader pays in confusion. Don't co-headline two different things.
- **Test:** After the hero, could the reader say in one sentence what Henway is?
- **Watch for:** hero blocks that try to be the app *and* the done-for-you studio at once.

### 7. Match voice to audience (B2B ≠ B2C)
Speak to each buyer's world. A solo builder cares about "your idea, shipped." A company hiring
the studio cares about "reliable, compliant, done." Don't use one tone for both.
- **Test:** Who is this block for, and does it sound like it was written for them?

### 8. One crisp hook + show it
Every page needs one sticky, ownable differentiator — and it should be *visible*, not just
stated. People need to see it.
- **Henway's hook:** "The step before you open any AI builder." Use it. Lead with it.
- **Show it:** product glimpses, named tools, screenshots — and distinctive brand assets (the
  chick 🐣). Ownable, memorable visuals are a real lever, not decoration.

## Per-page QA checklist

Run every page against this before shipping:

- [ ] **Sentence one** speaks to the reader's moment/desire (rule 1), not the company.
- [ ] The **category is clear** within the first beat — app, studio, or tool (rule 6).
- [ ] There's **one crisp hook**, and it's near the top (rule 8).
- [ ] The **enemy/status quo** is named before the promise (rule 2).
- [ ] **No jargon** a layperson would trip on (rule 3). (Also: no em/en dashes — house style.)
- [ ] Claims are backed by **3–5 tangible, pictureable** things (rule 4).
- [ ] **Nothing over-explained** — every sentence earns its place (rule 5).
- [ ] **Voice matches the audience** for that block (rule 7).
- [ ] There's a **visual** doing work, not just text (rule 8).

## Henway-specific reference (keep consistent everywhere)

- **The positioning (own this):** Henway is the **bridge / translation layer** between a person and
  AI. You describe your idea in plain words about work you already understand; Henway writes the
  expert prompt and picks the right tool. **No prompting, no code.** Tool-agnostic (the neutral
  layer above every AI builder), not locked to any one stack.
- **The hook (copy-paste standard):**
  "Turn what you already know into what AI can build. Henway is the bridge: you speak in plain words,
  and it writes the perfect prompt and picks the right tool. No prompting, no code."
- **The thesis (manifesto):** "You don't need to learn how to prompt, any more than you needed to
  learn to code. Every tool eventually hides its hardest part behind something familiar. Henway is
  that layer for AI."
- **Two paths, never blurred:**
  - **The app (B2C, lead with this):** free, 7 minutes, tells you the tool + writes your first
    prompt. Voice: personal, empowering, "your idea."
  - **The studio (B2B, secondary):** done-for-you builds for regulated industries. Voice:
    reliable, compliant, proven (IBM watsonx, Think stage).
- **Proof points:** 13 real build tools named; IBM Think keynote; IBM case study/blog; real
  client case studies.
- **Brand voice:** plain-spoken, confident, inclusive ("you don't need a technical co-founder or
  anyone's permission"). Mascot: the baby chick / "hatched" metaphor — use as a recurring device.
- **House style:** no em-dashes or en-dashes anywhere (use commas, colons, or parentheses).

_Not marketing dogma — a working checklist. Update it as we learn what converts._
